# Section-by-Section Review

## The Opening
**Verdict:** [Hooks immediately]
The opening is classic Shleifer: it uses a stark, numerical contrast to establish a puzzle. By comparing the 76-month recovery of the Great Recession with the 29-month recovery of COVID-19—despite the latter being three times as severe—the author makes the stakes immediately clear. 

*   **Praise:** "The worst labor market contractions since the Great Depression, yet their aftermath could hardly look more different." This is a punchy, declarative sentence that lands the point perfectly.
*   **Improvement:** The transition to "This paper asks why" is a bit of a cliché. You could make the "why" more active. 
*   **Suggested Rewrite:** Instead of "This paper asks why," try: "The contrast is a puzzle for standard theory: why does a smaller shock leave a decade of scars while a massive one vanishes in months?"

## Introduction
**Verdict:** [Shleifer-ready]
The introduction follows the gold standard arc. It moves from a vivid puzzle to a clear hypothesis (demand vs. supply) and provides specific preview results.

*   **Praise:** The second paragraph is excellent. It defines the "nature" of the shocks with Glaeser-like energy: demand "destroyed the incentive to hire" while supply was a "temporary shutdown." 
*   **Critique:** The contribution section (p. 3) gets a bit bogged down in a "shopping list" of citations. Shleifer usually weaves these into the narrative more tightly.
*   **Improvement:** Be more specific about the "what we find" in the preview. You have "0.8 percentage points lower employment," which is good, but tell us what that means for the average state.

## Background / Institutional Context
**Verdict:** [Vivid and necessary]
Section 2 does a great job of making the reader *see* the crisis. You mention specific "Sun Belt" states and the "anatomy" of the collapse.

*   **Katz sensibility:** You successfully ground the institutional details in human consequences: "workers lose skills, employer networks atrophy, and stigma reduces callback rates."
*   **Refinement:** The transition between 2.1 and 2.2 is strong, but make the policy contrast even sharper. You mention the ARRA arrived with a "lag" while the PPP was "direct." Highlight this timing as the reason the human "scars" were avoided.

## Data
**Verdict:** [Reads as narrative]
Unlike many papers that dump a list of sources, this section flows well. You describe the state-level panel as a tool to capture the "geographic distribution of recession severity."

*   **Specific feedback:** The description of the Bartik shock (p. 11) is intuitive. "Intuitively, the Bartik measure asks how much a state’s employment would have declined if each of its industries followed national trends." This is exactly how you explain math to a busy economist.
